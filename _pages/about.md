<div class="research-intro">
  <p class="research-lead">I study how developmental programs are altered in neuroblastoma, and how patient-derived stem cell models can help us understand where the disease begins and how it changes over time.</p>

  <p>I am currently a Ph.D. student in Tumor Biology in the <a href='https://ki.se/en/mtc/wilhelm-lab'>Wilhelm Group</a> at the <a href='https://ki.se/en/mtc/department-of-microbiology-tumor-and-cell-biology'>Department of Microbiology, Tumor and Cell Biology</a>, Karolinska Institutet. My work sits at the intersection of developmental biology, cancer modeling, and single-cell approaches.</p>
</div>

<div class="about-story-grid">
  <div class="story-panel story-panel-primary">
    <h3>What I work on</h3>
    <p>My main interest is building biologically meaningful models of childhood cancer. In particular, I use reprogrammed patient-derived stem cells to investigate how neuroblastoma may emerge from disrupted developmental trajectories.</p>
    <p>I am especially interested in questions about cell identity, plasticity, and the relationship between developmental state and tumor behavior.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="story-panel story-panel-secondary">
    <h3>How I approach it</h3>
    <p>My research combines stem cell culture, in vitro differentiation, histology, and single-cell omics. I am drawn to approaches that let us connect cellular phenotype with mechanism, rather than looking at tumors only as static end states.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="personal-note">
  <p>I trained in biological sciences at Southwest University and later completed an M.Sc. in Biomedicine at Karolinska Institutet. That path gradually moved me toward a question I still find compelling: how can developmental biology help us understand pediatric cancer more precisely and more meaningfully?</p>
</div>
